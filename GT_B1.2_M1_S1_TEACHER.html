📐Grammar — Tag Questions + Modals of Speculation — click to fill⏱ 8–10 min
📌 Teacher Note
Tag questions are about CONNECTION — they invite the other person into the conversation. Modals of speculation are about NUANCE — expressing degrees of certainty. Both are essential for natural, authentic debate at B1.2.
📐 Tag Questions + Modals of Speculation
TAG QUESTIONS
✅ positive + negative tag
Remote work is here to stay, isn't it?
✅ negative + positive tag
You haven't experienced burnout, have you?
❌ Remote work is good, isn't that?
MODALS OF SPECULATION
must be → almost certain (+)
That must be really stressful for the team.
can't be → almost certain (−)
It can't be easy managing 20 remote people.
might / could be → possible
This could be the future of all companies.
💡 Contrast expressions: whereas / while / on the other hand — used to compare two perspectives in debate.

Tag question rules
Positive clause → negative tag
Negative clause → positive tag
Modal verb → same modal in tag
❌ isn't that? / isn't this?
💡 Native speaker note
In real conversation, tag questions are used to invite agreement or check understanding — not just to test grammar. Rising intonation = genuine question. Falling intonation = seeking agreement.

📖Reading + Comprehension⏱ 10–12 min
💡 Click a sentence to highlight · Click underlined word for Spanish

I want to tell you about a conversation I had with a colleague a few years ago that I've never forgotten. We were both working in a company that, on paper, looked great: good salaries, modern offices, flexible hours. But something was wrong. People were exhausted. Nobody spoke up in meetings. Good employees kept leaving. One day, my colleague said something that stuck with me: "This place has a toxic culture,"Este lugar tiene una cultura tóxica "and the worst part is that nobody is allowed to say so."

That, I realized, was the definition of the absence of psychological safetyseguridad psicológica. The company wasn't bad because of one person. It was bad because the system itself punished honesty. People had started practicing what we now call quiet quittingrenuncia silenciosa — not because they were lazy, but because they had stopped believing their effort mattered. The burnoutagotamiento laboral wasn't dramatic. It was gradual. A slow draining of energy, creativity, and purpose.

I've thought about that experience a lot since then. And I've come to believe that a toxic culture doesn't just hurt individuals — it destroys companies from the inside. The good news? I've also worked in places where it was completely different. Where people felt safe to push backcuestionar decisiones, to disagree, to fail. The difference wasn't the ping-pong tables or the free coffee. It was whether the people at the top genuinely believed that their team's wellbeing was worth protecting. Remote work, hybrid models, flexible hours — none of it matters if the culture is broken. And a healthy culture doesn't build itself. Someone has to stand up fordefender it.

Let's start with the big question. Is work culture actually getting better, or is it just getting better at hiding its problems? — That's a tough one. And honestly, I think it depends on the company. In some organizations, the conversation around psychological safety and wellbeing is genuinely changing things. But in many others, it's still just branding. And the thing is, burnout doesn't announce itself. It creeps up on you. One day you're fine, the next you realize you haven't felt genuinely motivated in months. What about quiet quitting? Is that a real phenomenon or just a new name for something that's always existed? — I'd argue it's both. People have always disengaged from work when they feel undervalued. But what's new is that they're now naming it, discussing it publicly, and refusing to feel ashamed about it. That's a significant cultural shift, isn't it? — And do you think remote work has made things better or worse? — It's complicated. 🗣Colloquial English — B1.2 M1⏱ 5–7 min
📌 Teacher Note
These phrases are essential for sounding natural in debate and opinion conversations. At B1.2, the student should be integrating these into their speech automatically — not translating them.
🗣 Colloquial English — B1.2
"That's the thing…"
used to introduce the key point or the real problem in a discussion
That's the thing — quiet quitting isn't about laziness. It's about a broken system.
"To be honest…"
used to signal that you are about to say something direct or perhaps unexpected
To be honest, I think most companies talk about wellbeing but don't really mean it.
"Fair enough"
acknowledging that someone's point is valid, even if you don't fully agree
Fair enough — remote work isn't perfect. But neither is being stuck in an office.
"I get what you mean"
expressing that you understand someone's point, even before agreeing or disagreeing
I get what you mean, but I'd argue the problem goes deeper than just management style.

⚡Wrap-up & Error Correction⏱ 3–5 min
Common errors — B1.2 · Tag Questions & Modals
❌ Remote work is good, isn't that?
→
✅ Remote work is good, isn't it?
The tag question refers to the subject of the main clause. 'It' refers to 'remote work' — not 'that'.
❌ That must is really stressful.
→
✅ That must be really stressful.
Modals of speculation are always followed by 'be' when describing a state or situation.
❌ I'd argue that remote work it is better.
→
✅ I'd argue that remote work is better.
After a subordinate clause ('that'), use normal word order. No extra pronoun subject.
