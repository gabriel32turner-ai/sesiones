📖Reading — M1 Final Text⏱ 10–12 min
💡 Click a sentence to highlight · Click underlined word for Spanish

I want to close this module with a thought that connects everything we've discussed. We started with work culture — how companies treat people, and how people respond. We moved to social issues — the inequalities and tensions that shape the world we work in. We explored technology — the forces that are reshaping what work even means.

And we ended with the most personal question of all: what does success actually mean to you? What I hope you've noticed is that these topics are not separate. They are one conversation. The future of work, the quality of our societies, the tools we build and how we govern them, the meaning we find in what we do — all of it is interconnectedinterconectado. And here's what I'd contend: the ability to engage with these topics in English — to articulatearticular your stance, defend it under pressure, acknowledge its nuance, and still hold your conviction — that is not just a language skill.

It is a leadership skill. In B1.2, you are no longer just communicating in English. You are thinking in it. Not perfectly — nobody does. But authenticallyauténticamente. With your own perspectiveperspectiva and your own voice. And that, I'd argue — at the end of the day, in the long run, and not just in English class — is what actually matters.

I want to ask you something I've been thinking about since we started this module. If you could go back to the beginning of your career — knowing everything you know now — what would you do differently? — That's a tough one. I wouldn't change as much as people might expect. But I would have stood up for things earlier. There were moments when I saw an injustice — someone being underpaid, a toxic dynamic being ignored — and I told myself it wasn't my place. That can't be right, can it? It isn't somebody else's place. It's everybody's. — That resonates. What about the technology piece? AI is transforming everything, isn't it? How do you see that? — It cuts both ways. I'm not so sure that it's purely threatening — I think it depends on whether we treat it as a tool or a replacement. She argued last week in a panel that AI would make human empathy more valuable, not less. I'd contend that she was right.

And money — how has your relationship with money changed? — Completely. I used to think financial freedom meant having a lot. Now I think it means having enough to choose. In the long run, the moments that mattered weren't the big salaries. They were the moments of genuine connection and impact. I've come a long way from believing otherwise. — What would you bring up with young professionals who are just starting out? — Three things. First: stand up for inclusion from day one — not when it's convenient. Not only is it the right thing to do, but it also creates better organizations. Second: don't fall into the comparison trap. At the end of the day, you're not competing with anyone. You're building something that's yours. And third — to be honest — invest in your communication skills. Not just English. The ability to articulate what you believe, challenge what you disagree with, and acknowledge what you don't know — that, I'm convinced, is the skill of this century.

That's a remarkable perspective. — It's the one I wish I'd had sooner. Remote work, social change, AI, money — they're all just context. The real question — the one that doesn't change — is: do you know what you stand for? And can you say it clearly, in any language, to anyone in the room?

🗣 Colloquial English — B1.2
"That's the thing…"
introduces the key point — the real issue beneath the surface
That's the thing about remote work — it's not really about location, is it?
"It depends on…"
signals nuance — there's no single right answer
It depends on the company. Some genuinely change. Others just change the language.
"That makes total sense"
intellectual validation of someone's argument
That makes total sense — if the culture is broken, no perks will fix it.
"At the end of the day…"
clarifies what is most fundamentally true or important
At the end of the day, people don't leave companies — they leave managers.

❌ isn't that? / said me that / point that
→
✅ isn't IT? / told me / point OUT that
Three of the most common B1.2 errors — all grammatically related to structure, not vocabulary.
❌ If money is not an issue / If money doesn't exist…
→
✅ If money WEREN'T an issue…
Second conditional = imaginary. Past tense (weren't, not wasn't) always in the if-clause.
