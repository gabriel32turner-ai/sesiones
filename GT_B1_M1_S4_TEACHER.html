📐Grammar — Second Conditional in Business Contexts — click to fill⏱ 8–10 min
📌 Teacher Note
Focus on how Second Conditional works in business discussions — with both could and would, and with the result clause coming first for emphasis.
📐 Second Conditional — Variations in Business
Standard (if-clause first)
If the market shifted, we'd have to adapt.
If we merged, the culture would change.
Inverted (result first)
We'd have to adapt if the market shifted.
The culture would change if we merged.
With COULD (possibility) and MIGHT (less certain)
If we restructured, it could save 20% in costs. (possible result)
If we entered that market, we might face unexpected competition. (uncertain)
❌
If we would merge, things will change. · If they restructure, we would need to adapt.

📖Reading + Comprehension⏱ 10–12 min
💡 Click a sentence to highlight · Click underlined word for Spanish

In 2015, our industry was completely different. Nobody was talking about automationautomatizacion, AI, or remote-first companies. If you had told us then that within a decade, most of our team would work from home and AI would be writing our first drafts, we would have laughed. But here we are. And the companies that are thrivingprosperando are the ones that imagined the scenarios nobody else was willing to consider.

Think about this: if your company didn't adaptadaptarse its strategy in the next three years, what would happen? The honest answer, for most businesses, is: we'd fall behind. Significantly. The competitorscompetidores who would scaleescalar fastest are the ones already building for a world that doesn't exist yet. So here's the question worth asking: if you could do one thing differently right now — not next year, not when the budget is ready — what would that be?

Because in five years, you'll either look back and say: 'I'm glad we pivotedpivoteamos.' Or you'll look back and say: 'If only we had acted sooner.' The Second Conditional isn't just grammar. It's a way of thinking. And companies that think hypothetically — that imagine failure before it happens — are the ones that survivesobreviven.

🗣Colloquial English — B1 M1⏱ 5–7 min
📌 Teacher Note
These phrases are used in business discussions about scenarios, risk, and strategy. Very natural in meetings and brainstorming sessions.
🗣 Colloquial English — B1
"Let's cross that bridge when we come to it"
deal with a problem only when it actually happens, not before
If that regulation changes, we'll adapt. Let's cross that bridge when we come to it.
"Worst case scenario"
the most negative possible outcome of a situation
If we launch and it fails, what's the worst case scenario? Can we recover?
"Hedge your bets"
reduce risk by supporting more than one option at the same time
If I were you, I'd hedge my bets — don't put all your resources into one strategy.
"Ahead of the curve"
more advanced than others, doing something before it becomes common
If we invested in AI now, we'd be well ahead of the curve when it becomes standard.

⚡Wrap-up & Error Correction⏱ 3–5 min
Common errors — B1 · Business Hypotheticals
❌ If the market will change, we adapt our strategy.
→
✅ If the market changed, we would adapt our strategy.
Imaginary future scenario = Second Conditional. Past simple + would, not present + present.
❌ If we could expand, we would have entered that market already.
→
✅ If we could expand, we would enter that market.
'Would have' is Third Conditional (past regret). Second Conditional talks about present/future hypotheticals.
❌ The company might would face challenges.
→
✅ The company might face challenges.
'Might' and 'would' are both modals. You can't combine them. Use one: 'would face' or 'might face'.
