🔵Warm-up⏱ 5–7 min
THIS SESSION · The $10 Million Question

Teacher says: "You just received $10 million. You can't spend it on yourself for 5 years. What do you do with your time?" Student responds freely for 2 minutes. Then discuss: What does the answer reveal about what you actually value? Then reverse: "You lost everything. You have to start from scratch at 35. What's your plan?"

Why: These hypotheticals reveal genuine values — exactly the territory B1.2 is designed to explore.

📐Grammar — Second Conditional Fluido + Hedging Language — click to fill⏱ 8–10 min
📌 Teacher Note
At B1.2, Second Conditional is reviewed as a fluency tool, not new grammar. The focus is hedging — the ability to present views without being absolute, which is essential for sophisticated debate.
📐 2nd Conditional + Hedging Language
2nd Conditional — Fluent Use
If money weren't an issue, what would you do?
I wouldn't stay in this role if I didn't believe in the mission.
If success were only about money, very few people would feel successful.
Hedging Language
It could be argued that…
Some people would say…
In a way…
I'm convinced that…
I strongly believe…
💡 Hedging = presenting your view with intellectual humility. It signals sophistication — not weakness. Native speakers hedge constantly in serious conversations.

📖Reading + Comprehension⏱ 10–12 min
💡 Click a sentence to highlight · Click underlined word for Spanish

I want to share something I've never told anyone in a professional context. For a long time, I defined success by one metric: salary. If the number went up, I was succeeding. If it didn't, something was wrong. I pursued financial freedomlibertad financiera with the kind of focus that leaves very little room for anything else. I worked in the rat racecompetencia agotadora del éxito convencional, and I was good at it. Promotions came. Recognition came. And then, slowly, something else came too: a deep and quiet sense of emptinessvacío.

It could be argued that I was experiencing what psychologists call imposter syndromesíndrome del impostor — the feeling that despite external success, something inside doesn't believe you deserve it. But I think it was something simpler: I had been measuring the wrong thing. I hadn't been pursuing fulfillmentrealización personal. I had been pursuing the appearance of it. The comparison traptrampa de la comparación had become my operating system. I measured my worth by what others had, not by what I actually valued.

Some people would say this is just a midlife crisis dressed in philosophical language. Fair enough. But I'd strongly argue that the question 'what does success mean to you?' is one of the most important questions a professional can ask — and one of the least often asked. If money weren't a constraint, what would you actually do with your time? The answer to that question tells you everything. Not because you should quit your job and follow your passion — that's not always possible or wise. But because knowing the answer changes how you make decisions. It changes what you say yes to. And increasingly, what you have the courage to say no to.

🗣 Colloquial English — B1.2
"Not really, no"
a natural, direct way to say no without being blunt or rude
Not really, no — I don't think money is what drives me anymore.
"Money doesn't grow on trees"
a common saying meaning money has to be worked for and is limited
I understand the appeal of entrepreneurship, but money doesn't grow on trees.
"In the long run…"
referring to what happens or matters over a long period of time
In the long run, the decisions you make in your thirties define your fifties.
"At the end of the day…"
used to introduce what is most fundamentally true or important
At the end of the day, we all want to feel that our work meant something.

⚡Wrap-up & Error Correction⏱ 3–5 min
Common errors — B1.2 · Hedging & 2nd Conditional
❌ If money is not an issue, I would choose a different career.
→
✅ If money weren't an issue, I would choose a different career.
Second conditional = imaginary. Use past tense in the if-clause: 'weren't', not 'is not'.
❌ I am convinced that fulfillment is important.
→
✅ I'm convinced that fulfillment matters more than status.
'I am convinced' is grammatically correct but sounds stiff. 'I'm convinced' is more natural in speech and writing.
❌ It could be argued that money doesn't matters.
→
✅ It could be argued that money doesn't matter.
After 'doesn't', always use the base verb. 'Matters' → 'doesn't MATTER'. Third person singular only applies with positive simple present.
