📖Reading + Comprehension⏱ 10–12 min
💡 Click a sentence to highlight · Click underlined word for Spanish

I want to ask you something that might feel uncomfortable — but that's exactly why it's worth asking. When was the last time your company openly discussed the gender pay gapbrecha salarial de género? Or acknowledged that unconscious biassesgo inconsciente might be shaping hiring decisions? Or created a space where someone could talk about their mental healthsalud mental without fear of judgment?

For most organizations, the honest answer is: not recently, if ever. And yet, the research is clear. Companies that take inclusioninclusión seriously — not as a PR exercise, but as a genuine organizational commitment — consistently outperform those that don't. Not only do diverse teams bring broader perspectives, but they also make better decisions. The data is no longer debatable. Furthermore, there is the human dimension. When people feel a sense of belongingpertenencia, they stay longer, contribute more, and take greater risks with their ideas.

The challenge is that inclusion requires discomfort. It means bringing up topics that have historically been avoided. It means standing up for people whose voices have been systematically excluded. It means looking into your own biases — not because you are a bad person, but because you are a human one. In order to build truly inclusive organizations, we don't need perfect people. We need honest ones. And that, in the end, is what makes all the difference.

So I want to put a provocative question on the table: do you think DEI policies are actually working? — I think it depends enormously on how they're implemented. When companies look into their data honestly and come up with concrete targets, yes, things change. But when it's just a box-ticking exercise — a poster on the wall and a one-day training — it achieves almost nothing. Furthermore, it can actually create backlash, because people feel like it's being imposed rather than built together. — That's a fair point. And what about the gender pay gap specifically? Is that something companies are genuinely addressing? — Not enough. Not only are women still paid less on average, but they're also underrepresented in senior leadership. In order to change that, you need structural interventions — not just awareness. Mentorship programs. Transparent salary bands. Promoting women into stretch roles. It doesn't happen by itself.

And mental health — is that a social issue or a workplace issue? — Both, clearly. As a result of years of stigma, people have learned not to speak up about mental health at work. The culture of 'leave your problems at home' has caused enormous damage. I've seen brilliant people burn out and leave companies because nobody stood up for a more humane approach. — So what would you bring up first if you were advising a company right now? — Psychological safety, without question. Until people feel safe to speak up, nothing else works. Not DEI initiatives, not mental health programs, not inclusion training. It all depends on whether people believe they can be honest without consequences. And building that trust takes years. But it's the only foundation that works.

That's a compelling point. Do you see generational differences in how people approach these issues? — Absolutely. Younger workers — millennials, Gen Z — are much less willing to deal with toxic environments or dismiss social issues as irrelevant to work. As a result, companies that don't evolve are losing the best talent. Whereas older generations tended to separate work from social values, younger people expect alignment. They want to work for companies that stand up for something. And I think that's not a problem — I think that's progress.

🗣Colloquial English — B1.2 M1⏱ 5–7 min
📌 Teacher Note
These phrases are essential for discussing complex social topics without sounding either dismissive or preachy. They signal nuance — one of the most important qualities in authentic debate.
🗣 Colloquial English — B1.2
"It depends on…"
used to introduce nuance — acknowledging that there's no single answer
It depends on the company. Some genuinely invest in inclusion. Others just talk about it.
"The way I see it…"
expressing a personal perspective clearly and confidently
The way I see it, the gender pay gap is a symptom of a much deeper cultural problem.
"That's a tough one"
acknowledging that a question is genuinely difficult before answering it
That's a tough one. I can see the arguments on both sides.
"I see both sides"
expressing that you understand multiple perspectives before giving your own
I see both sides — DEI policies can create real change, but they can also create resentment if poorly implemented.

⚡Wrap-up & Error Correction⏱ 3–5 min
Common errors — B1.2 · Phrasal Verbs & Linkers
❌ We need to bring up the gap of pay.
→
✅ We need to bring up the pay gap.
Phrasal verbs and their objects follow natural English word order. The noun phrase stays together.
❌ Furthermore, it is also important to mention that…
→
✅ Furthermore, it is important to mention that…
'Furthermore' already signals addition — 'also' is redundant. Don't combine addition linkers.
❌ Not only the women are paid less, but also they are underrepresented.
→
✅ Not only are women paid less, but they are also underrepresented.
'Not only' triggers inversion: verb comes before subject. 'Not only ARE women' — not 'Not only the women are'.
