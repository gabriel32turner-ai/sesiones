THIS SESSION · Wish List at Work

Teacher says: "Complete these 3 starters honestly — no thinking, just say what comes to mind: 1) I wish my job had more… 2) I'd rather work with people who… 3) If I could change one thing about how we communicate, I would…" Student responds. Then discuss: how realistic would these wishes be? What would actually need to change?

Why: I wish and I'd rather are most natural when they connect to real, felt preferences — not invented scenarios.

📐Grammar — Would, I'd Rather & Wish — click to fill⏱ 8–10 min
📌 Teacher Note
Three structures, one idea: imagining things differently. Wish + past simple is the subtlest — students often confuse it with simple present. Drill the past simple after wish.
📐 Would, I'd Rather & Wish
WOULD
Hypothetical result
I would change the process.
She wouldn't accept that.
Would you take the job?
I'D RATHER
Express preference
I'd rather work from home.
I'd rather NOT attend.
I'd rather have fewer meetings.
WISH + PAST
Desire for change
I wish I had more time.
I wish the process were simpler.
She wishes she worked closer.
💡 Wish + past simple = I want reality to be different. Like a mini Second Conditional: I wish I had more time = If I had more time, I'd be happier.

Key rule: Wish + past
I wish I had more time. (NOT have)
I wish it were easier. (NOT is)
Wish = mini Second Conditional
💡 I'd rather vs I prefer
'I'd rather' is followed by a base verb (without to). 'I prefer to' is followed by infinitive (with to). Both express preference but 'I'd rather' is more casual and spontaneous.

I wanted to ask you something I've been thinking about. If you could go back ten years, what would you do differently? — That's a big question. Honestly, I wish I had trusted my instincts more. Early in my career, I often knew what the right move was, but I'd wait for someone else to validate it. I'd rather have been more decisive. — I completely relate to that. 🗣Colloquial English — B1 M1⏱ 5–7 min
📌 Teacher Note
These phrases all connect to the theme of wishing things were different or expressing preferences. Drill each one: student uses it in a real sentence about their job or life.
🗣 Colloquial English — B1
"I'd rather"
express a preference — more casual and spontaneous than 'I prefer'
I'd rather not have this conversation over email — can we talk?
"That ship has sailed"
it's too late for that now, that opportunity or option no longer exists
I wish I had started learning English earlier, but that ship has sailed.
"Grass is always greener"
people always think things would be better in a different situation
I keep wishing I had a different job, but the grass is always greener, right?
"Be careful what you wish for"
sometimes getting what you want brings unexpected problems
I always wished for a bigger team — be careful what you wish for, right?

⚡Wrap-up & Error Correction⏱ 3–5 min
Common errors — B1 · Would / I'd Rather / Wish
❌ I wish I have more time.
→
✅ I wish I had more time.
After 'wish', always use past simple. Wish + present = common error. Think of it as a mini Second Conditional.
❌ I'd rather to work from home.
→
✅ I'd rather work from home.
'I'd rather' is followed by base verb WITHOUT 'to'. Not 'I'd rather to work' — just 'I'd rather work'.
❌ I wish I would change careers.
→
✅ I wish I could change careers.
Don't use 'would' after 'wish' to talk about present wishes. Use could/were/had.
