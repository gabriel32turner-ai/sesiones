Reporting & Debate Verbs
Word / PhraseDefinitionExample
argueto present a position with evidence and reasoningShe argued that social media does more harm than good to democratic discourse.
claimto state something as a fact, often without full proofHe claimed that AI would replace 40% of jobs within a decade.
contendto assert something firmly as your position in a debateI would contend that data privacy is a human rights issue, not just a technical one.
point outto draw attention to a fact or issueIt's important to point out that automation also creates new kinds of jobs.
acknowledgeto recognize or admit the existence of somethingWe need to acknowledge that not everyone has equal access to technology.
challengeto question or dispute a belief or claimShe challenged the idea that AI is inherently neutral.
highlightto emphasize or draw attention to something importantThe report highlighted the alarming gap in digital skills across generations.
speculateto form opinions without full evidenceIt's hard to speculate about the long-term effects of AI on society.

📌 Teacher Note
At B1.2, reported speech is a debate tool — not a grammar rule to recite. The student should use it naturally to introduce other perspectives before challenging or supporting them.
📐 Reported Speech in Debate
Reporting Verbs
argue — she argued that AI creates inequality
claim — he claimed that data is the new oil
contend — critics contend that algorithms are biased
highlight — the report highlighted that…
point out — she pointed out that…
Debate Structure
1. Report the claim
2. Signal your position
3. Add evidence
4. Invite response with tag
She argued that AI is a threat. I'd contend that it's also an opportunity — don't you think?
❌
She said me that AI will destroy jobs. · He argued that AI is destroying jobs now. (tense shift needed)

📖Reading + Comprehension⏱ 10–12 min
💡 Click a sentence to highlight · Click underlined word for Spanish

Let me be direct: I believe we are living through the most significant technological transformation in human history. And yet, the conversation about its consequences is still surprisingly shallow. Critics have argued that automationautomatización will destroy millions of jobs. Some researchers have contended that up to 40% of current roles will be fundamentally transformed within a decade. But others have pointed out that every previous industrial revolution also created fear — and ultimately created more jobs than it eliminated.

It's hard to say who is right. Clearly, the algorithmalgoritmo knows more about your preferences than most of your friends. That can't be neutral. There's no doubt that doomscrollingconsumo obsesivo de noticias negativas is affecting mental health at scale — the data is unambiguous. And yet, the same technology connects people across continents, gives voice to those previously unheard, and has democratized access to knowledge. I'd contend that the problem is not technology itself. The problem is that we haven't decided — as societies — what we want it to do.

We've allowed surveillancevigilancia capitalism to grow without sufficient regulation. We've widened the digital dividebrecha digital while calling it progress. The tech CEO argued that innovation must come first, and regulation later. I would challenge that. By the time we regulate, the damage is done. The question isn't whether AI is a threat or an opportunity. It's both. The real question is: who decides? And right now, it's mostly the people who benefit from it most.

🗣Colloquial English — B1.2 M1⏱ 5–7 min
📌 Teacher Note
These phrases signal intellectual engagement — they show the student is thinking critically, not just reciting opinions. At B1.2, sounding thoughtful is as important as being grammatically correct.
🗣 Colloquial English — B1.2
"That makes total sense"
expressing that an argument or explanation is logical and convincing
That makes total sense — if companies don't invest in upskilling, they're creating their own talent crisis.
"I'm not so sure about that"
expressing measured doubt about a claim without dismissing it
I'm not so sure about that. The data on remote work productivity is still quite mixed.
"That's a bit of a stretch"
indicating that an argument goes further than the evidence supports
Saying AI will replace all creative jobs by 2030 — that's a bit of a stretch, honestly.
"It cuts both ways"
acknowledging that something has both positive and negative implications
AI in recruitment cuts both ways — it removes some bias but introduces new kinds of it.

⚡Wrap-up & Error Correction⏱ 3–5 min
Common errors — B1.2 · Reported Speech in Debate
❌ She said me that AI will destroy all jobs.
→
✅ She said (that) AI would destroy all jobs. / She told me that AI would destroy all jobs.
'Say' has no object. 'Tell' requires an object (told ME). And past tense: will → would.
❌ He argued that AI is destroying jobs currently.
→
✅ He argued that AI was destroying jobs.
In reported speech, present continuous → past continuous. 'is destroying' becomes 'was destroying'.
❌ Critics have pointed that the gap is widening.
→
✅ Critics have pointed out that the gap is widening.
'Point out' is a phrasal verb — the particle 'out' is required. 'Point that' is not correct.
