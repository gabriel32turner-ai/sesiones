🔵Warm-up⏱ 5–7 min
THIS SESSION · The Advice Hot Seat

Teacher presents 4 workplace dilemmas one by one. Student gives advice using Second Conditional. The rule: every piece of advice must start with "If I were you…" or "If I were in your position…" Teacher plays devil's advocate: pushes back on the advice to generate a full conversation.

Why: giving advice to others is the fastest way to internalize "If I were you" — it becomes automatic through real use.
DILEMMA 1
My manager takes credit for my work in front of senior leadership.
DILEMMA 2
I've been offered a promotion, but it means relocating to another city.
DILEMMA 3
A colleague is clearly struggling but refuses to ask for help.
DILEMMA 4
I think our company's strategy is heading in the wrong direction — but I'm not the CEO.

📖Reading + Comprehension⏱ 10–12 min
💡 Click a sentence to highlight · Click underlined word for Spanish

One of the most valuable things a mentor ever said to me was: 'Stop waiting for permission.' I was in my early thirties, in a good job, but feeling stuck. I kept thinking: 'If I had more experience, I'd apply for that role. If I had better connections, I'd reach out. If I were more confident, I'd speak up in those meetings.' My mentor looked at me and said: 'If I were you, I'd stop making conditions for myself. You already have what you need.'

It sounds simple. But hearing it from someone I respectedrespetaba made it hit differently. She added: 'What I'd do, if I were in your shoes, is write down the one thing you'd do if you knew you couldn't fail. Then do that thing.' I said: 'That's easy for you to say.' And she said: 'I know. But here's the thing — it was hard for me too. I wish someone had told me this at your age.' I thought about her advice for weeks. And eventually, I took it.

I applied for the role. I reached out to the people I'd been afraid to contact. I started speaking up. Not because I suddenly felt ready — I didn't. But because someone told me: 'If I were you, I wouldn't wait until you do.' That advice changed the trajectory of my career. And whenever someone asks me for advice now, I pass it on: don't wait for the conditions to be perfect. They never will be.

So I've been offered the role, but I'm honestly terrified. I don't know if I'm ready. — Can I be direct with you? — Please. — If I were in your position, I wouldn't be thinking about whether I'm ready. I'd be thinking about whether I want it. Those are two completely different questions. — That's a good point. — What I'd do is separate the fear from the decision. Fear tells you it matters. It doesn't tell you it's wrong. — But what if I fail? — And what if you succeed? You have to give both possibilities equal weight. If I were you, I'd ask yourself: what's the cost of NOT taking this? — That's something I haven't really considered.

What would you recommend practically? What are the actual first steps? — If I were advising a friend in your situation, I'd say: first, stop framing it as 'am I ready' and start framing it as 'what do I need to learn?' Those are very different conversations with yourself. Second, I wouldn't accept without negotiating the terms. Make sure you have the resources, the team, and the clarity you need to succeed. And third — and this is the one most people skip — have a conversation with your current manager before you decide. Let them know you're being considered. You'd be surprised what that conversation opens up. — I wouldn't have thought of that last one. — Most people wouldn't. But if I were in your shoes, I'd want to know if there were possibilities I hadn't seen yet before committing.

What would you do if you were genuinely unsure? — If I were genuinely unsure, I'd give myself a deadline. Not months — a week. I'd write down every concern, every excitement, every question. Then I'd look at the list and ask: which side would I regret more? — And if they were equal? — Then I'd take the risk. Because if I were being honest — safe decisions rarely change careers. Brave ones do. — That's exactly what I needed to hear.

⚡Wrap-up & Error Correction⏱ 3–5 min
Common errors — B1 · Giving Advice
❌ If I was you, I'd talk to HR.
→
✅ If I were you, I'd talk to HR.
'Were' is correct for all persons in formal Second Conditional — including I, he, she, it. 'If I was' is heard informally but 'were' is always safe.
❌ I'd recommend you to speak directly.
→
✅ I'd recommend speaking directly. / I'd recommend that you speak directly.
'Recommend' is followed by -ing or a that-clause — NOT 'recommend + to + verb'.
❌ What I'd do is to ask for a meeting.
→
✅ What I'd do is ask for a meeting.
After 'What I'd do is…', use the base verb WITHOUT 'to'. The 'to' is already implied.
